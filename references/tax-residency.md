# Tax Residency & Digital Nomad Guide for Solo Founders

## The Core Question: Where Are YOU Taxed?

Your company's tax is one thing. YOUR personal tax is another. As a solo founder, they're deeply connected because most solo structures are pass-through (LLC) or you're the sole shareholder.

**Three separate concepts:**
1. **Company incorporation** — where your business is registered
2. **Company tax residency** — where your business pays corporate tax (usually where it's managed)
3. **Personal tax residency** — where YOU pay personal income tax

## The 183-Day Rule (And Why It's Not That Simple)

Most countries use the 183-day rule: if you spend ≥183 days in a country during a tax year, you're likely a tax resident. BUT:

- Some countries count ANY partial day (arrive at 11pm = 1 day)
- Some use calendar year, others fiscal year (UK: April 6)
- Some have additional tests beyond days (family, property, "center of vital interests")
- Some countries claim you even with <183 days if you have a "permanent home" available
- **US citizens are ALWAYS US tax residents regardless of where they live**

## Critical Tax Traps for Solo Founders

### Trap 1: US Citizens — You Can't Escape US Tax
- US taxes worldwide income regardless of where you live
- Must file US return even if living abroad permanently
- **FEIE**: Exclude up to ~$126,500 (2024) of earned income if bona fide resident abroad
- **FTC**: Credit foreign taxes paid against US tax
- **CFC rules**: If you own >50% of a foreign corporation, Subpart F income and GILTI apply
  - Form 5471 mandatory: $10,000+ penalty per year if missed
  - GILTI tax on foreign company profits even if not distributed
- **Solution for US citizens**: US LLC (pass-through) is often simplest; avoids CFC complexity

### Trap 2: Permanent Establishment (PE) Risk
- If you work from a co-working space in Country X for >90-183 days, your company may create a PE there
- PE = your company owes corporate tax in that country
- **Freelancers/consultants**: "Service PE" triggered by extended project work in a country
- **Mitigation**: Track days carefully, don't sign contracts locally, use client premises carefully

### Trap 3: Company Tax Residency ≠ Incorporation
- A company incorporated in Singapore but managed from your laptop in Portugal may be tax resident in PORTUGAL
- Tax authorities look at where "central management and control" happens
- **Solution**: Hold board meetings (even virtual) in the incorporation country, keep minutes

### Trap 4: "Nowhere" Tax Residency Doesn't Work
- If you travel constantly and claim no tax residency, your HOME country may still claim you
- Tax authorities look at: passport, bank accounts, property, family, "center of vital interests"
- **Solution**: Deliberately establish tax residency in ONE favorable country

### Trap 5: Social Security Double Contribution
- Many countries require social security contributions from residents
- Without totalization agreements, you may pay into two systems
- EU countries have coordination rules; US has agreements with ~30 countries

## Recommended Strategies by Profile

### Profile A: US Citizen, Digital Nomad
- **Structure**: Wyoming/Delaware LLC (pass-through)
- **Tax strategy**: FEIE ($126K exclusion) + FTC for anything above
- **Residency**: Establish bona fide residence in a foreign country (need to be there 330+ days)
- **Banking**: Mercury + Wise
- **Avoid**: Foreign corporations (triggers CFC/GILTI complexity)

### Profile B: Non-US, Digital Nomad, Revenue <$100K
- **Structure**: Wyoming LLC (0% US tax for non-residents)
- **Tax residency**: Establish in territorial tax country (Panama, Georgia, Paraguay)
- **Banking**: Mercury + Wise
- **Total tax**: Near 0% legally
- **Cost**: ~$1,500/year all-in

### Profile C: Non-US, Living in One Country, Revenue >$100K
- **Structure**: Depends on customer location
  - US customers → Wyoming LLC or Delaware LLC
  - EU customers → Estonia OÜ or UK Ltd
  - Asia customers → Singapore Pte. Ltd.
- **Tax residency**: Your country of residence (file there)
- **Optimization**: Use DTA between your country and company country

### Profile D: Founder Wanting Zero Tax Legally
- **Structure**: Dubai Freezone (or Wyoming LLC)
- **Tax residency**: UAE (0% personal income tax)
- **Requirement**: Actually live in UAE (get Emirates ID, establish life there)
- **Banking**: Local UAE bank + Wise
- **Total tax**: 9% corporate on >AED 375K + 0% personal = effective ~5-8%
- **NOT for US citizens** (still owe US tax)

## Trap 6: Exit Tax (Departure Tax)

When leaving a high-tax country, the act of departure itself can trigger a large one-time tax bill. This is often the single largest tax event in a founder's life.

| Country | Rule | Trigger | Key Threshold | Deferral? |
|---------|------|---------|---------------|-----------|
| Germany | § 6 AStG | Deemed disposal of shares in foreign companies on departure | >1% shareholding held for 5+ years | Yes — 5-year deferral within EU/EEA; installments possible |
| USA | HEART Act (2008) | Mark-to-market on all worldwide assets for covered expatriates renouncing citizenship or long-term residency | Net worth >$2M OR average annual net income tax >$190K (2024, indexed) | No deferral; tax due on deemed gain as of day before expatriation |
| Australia | CGT Event I1 | Deemed disposal of all taxable Australian property on becoming non-resident | All assets with Australian situs | Main residence exemption may still apply; some assets can be excluded |
| Canada | Departure Tax | Deemed disposition of all property at FMV on the date you cease to be resident | All property except excluded types (e.g., Canadian real estate, RRSPs) | Payment deferral available if you post acceptable security with CRA |
| France | Exit Tax (Article 167 bis CGI) | Applies to direct/indirect shareholdings above threshold | Shareholdings worth >€800K OR representing >50% of company profits | 5-year deferral (EU/EEA); 2-year deferral (other countries); holding 2+ years before departure may reduce base |
| Netherlands | Conservatory Assessment | 10-year lookback on substantial interest holdings | >5% direct or indirect shareholding | Tax assessed at departure; collected on actual disposal within 10 years |

**Practical advice:**
- Plan your departure 12–24 months in advance — most mitigation strategies require lead time
- Get a written tax opinion BEFORE you move, not after
- Germany § 6 deferral only works if you move to another EU/EEA country; moving to Dubai resets the clock and the bill comes due in installments over 5 years
- US covered expatriates: the $800K per-person lifetime exclusion applies to the mark-to-market gain; gifts and bequests to US persons after expatriation are taxed at 40%
- Australia: electing to continue to be taxed as a resident (temporary resident exit) is sometimes worth it for assets with large unrealized gains
- Canada: the security arrangement delays cash outflow but does not reduce the tax; plan liquidity carefully
- Exit tax is often the largest single tax event in a founder's life — treat it accordingly

## Split Tax Year (Mid-Year Relocation)

Moving mid-year creates a period where two countries may both claim you. The rules vary dramatically by jurisdiction.

### Countries WITH Formal Split-Year Treatment

| Country | How It Works | Key Rule |
|---------|-------------|----------|
| UK | Statutory Residence Test (SRT) allows split-year in defined "cases" | 8 cases; must meet a case AND be UK resident for the year overall; each half taxed separately |
| Germany | Tax year = calendar year; income prorated to period of residence | Unlimited tax liability ends on date of Abmeldung; limited liability continues for German-source income |
| Australia | Partial-year resident status recognized; foreign income not taxed in non-resident portion | ATO determines residency date based on facts; lodging a departure date is critical |

### Countries WITHOUT Split-Year Treatment

Most countries treat you as either resident or non-resident for the **entire** calendar year:
- **USA**: No split year for citizens (always filing). For green card holders / substantial presence test, the "dual-status" return applies but creates complexity.
- **Singapore**: No formal split; authorities consider tax residency for the full year.
- **UAE**: No income tax, so split year is irrelevant.
- **Panama / Georgia / Paraguay**: Territorial systems; foreign income not taxed regardless.

### Practical Steps for the Transition Year

1. **Formal deregistration**: Get paper proof — Abmeldung (Germany), departure notification (Australia), change of address with tax authority (UK HMRC form P85)
2. **Establish new residency immediately**: Signed lease, local bank account, utility bill — all dated as early as possible in the new country
3. **Get a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC)** from the new country as soon as you qualify (often requires 183 days; some countries issue provisional TRC)
4. **File returns in BOTH countries** for the transition year — even if you owe nothing in one of them
5. **Claim DTA tie-breaker** if both countries assert full-year residency — the treaty hierarchy is: permanent home → center of vital interests → habitual abode → nationality → mutual agreement

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Hurts |
|---------|-------------|
| Leaving in late November/December | Most countries look at the full calendar year; one month abroad rarely severs residency |
| Not registering in the new country promptly | Creates a gap — tax authorities in both countries see you as "not really there" |
| Assuming Airbnb stays count as "habitual abode" | Courts and tax authorities look for durable connection — short-term accommodation rarely qualifies |
| Filing in only one country for the transition year | The other country may issue an assessment years later with interest and penalties |

**Timeline:** Budget 3–6 months of "overlap" where both countries have potential claims. The TRC from the new country is your primary defense.

## Marriage & Tax Residency Changes

Your partner's nationality and domicile can fundamentally alter your tax position — in ways most advisors miss until it's too late.

### Marrying a US Citizen (Non-US Spouse)

The non-US spouse does **not** automatically become a US taxpayer, but the choices you make on your first joint return lock in your treatment for years.

| Filing Status | Effect on Non-US Spouse | Trade-off |
|--------------|------------------------|-----------|
| Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) | Non-US spouse treated as US resident for tax purposes; all worldwide income of both spouses included | Access to higher standard deduction ($29,200 in 2024) and lower rates; but spouse's foreign income is now US-taxable |
| Married Filing Separately (MFS) | Non-US spouse NOT treated as US resident; their foreign income excluded | Lose many deductions and credits; often higher effective rate for US spouse |
| MFJ election (IRC §6013(g)) | One-time irrevocable election to treat non-US spouse as resident alien permanently | Rarely advisable unless both spouses intend to be US-based long-term |

**Additional compliance triggered by marrying a US citizen:**
- **FBAR (FinCEN 114)**: Joint foreign accounts are reportable by the US spouse if aggregate balance >$10,000 at any point in the year; $10,000 penalty per unreported account per year
- **FATCA (Form 8938)**: Spouse's foreign financial assets may need to be reported; thresholds are $50,000 (single) / $100,000 (MFJ) for US residents; higher for non-US residents
- **Gift tax**: Gifts between spouses where one is non-US citizen are limited to $185,000/year (2024, indexed) before gift tax applies — vs. unlimited marital deduction between two US citizens

### Marrying Into a High-Tax Country

Marriage creates new "center of vital interests" facts in the destination country's residency test:

| Factor | How Marriage Changes It |
|--------|------------------------|
| Family ties | Spouse resident in Country X is strong evidence of vital interests there |
| Permanent home | Shared residence in Country X satisfies "permanent home available" test in most DTAs |
| Social ties | Joint bank accounts, children enrolled in schools, memberships — all accumulate quickly |

**Practical implication:** You can inadvertently become tax resident in a high-tax country simply by spending weekends there with a resident spouse, even if you maintain your "official" residency elsewhere.

### Divorce & Business Ownership

| Scenario | Risk |
|----------|------|
| LLC membership in community property state (CA, TX, WA, AZ, ID, LA, NV, NM, WI) | Spouse may automatically own 50% of LLC membership interest acquired during marriage; divorce splits it |
| Foreign company shares acquired during marriage | Some countries (e.g., France with "régime de communauté") treat business equity as jointly owned; forced sale risk on divorce |
| Founder's vesting schedule | Unvested shares at time of divorce may still be counted in the marital estate by courts; get a QDRO or equivalent order to segregate |

**Mitigation:** A prenuptial or postnuptial agreement specifying separate property treatment for business interests is the cleanest protection in community property jurisdictions. Consult a family law attorney AND a tax attorney — the tax consequences of property transfers in divorce are separate from the ownership question.
