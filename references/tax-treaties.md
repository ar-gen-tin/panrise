# Key Tax Treaties for Solo Founders

## Why DTAs Matter for You

A Double Tax Agreement (DTA) prevents you from paying tax twice on the same income. As a solo founder, DTAs matter when:
- Your company is in Country A and you live in Country B
- You receive dividends, royalties, or service fees across borders
- A client withholds tax from your payments

## The 5 Routes That Matter Most

### Route 1: US LLC → Anywhere (Pass-Through)
- **How it works**: US LLC doesn't pay US tax (pass-through to owner)
- **DTA relevance**: LOW — no corporate-level tax event
- **Your tax**: Only where YOU are personally tax-resident
- **Key benefit**: No withholding on most payments to LLC

### Route 2: Singapore → You (Dividends)
- **Singapore dividend WHT**: 0% (Singapore never withholds on dividends)
- **No DTA needed** for dividend extraction
- **Your personal tax**: Depends on your tax residency country
- **Key benefit**: Cleanest profit extraction in Asia

### Route 3: Estonia → EU (Retained Profits)
- **Estonia corporate tax**: 0% while retained
- **Distribution tax**: 20% when profits paid out
- **Within EU**: No additional withholding (Parent-Subsidiary Directive)
- **To non-EU**: Check DTA (most reduce to 5-10%)

### Route 4: Hong Kong → Anywhere
- **HK dividend WHT**: 0%
- **Offshore profits**: 0% HK tax
- **Combined**: If income is genuinely offshore, effective tax near 0%
- **Watch**: FSIE rules (2023+) require substance for passive income exemption

### Route 5: Dubai → Anywhere
- **UAE dividend WHT**: 0%
- **UAE personal tax**: 0%
- **Corporate tax**: 9% on >AED 375K
- **DTA network**: 130+ treaties
- **Key benefit**: Low corporate + zero personal = lowest legal combined rate

## Common DTA Rates (Dividend Withholding)

| From → To | Rate | Notes |
|-----------|------|-------|
| Singapore → any | 0% | Singapore never withholds |
| Hong Kong → any | 0% | HK never withholds |
| Estonia → EU | 0% | EU directive |
| Estonia → non-EU | 7-15% | Check specific DTA |
| US LLC → owner | N/A | Pass-through, no WHT event |
| UK → any | 0% | UK doesn't withhold on dividends |
| Dubai/UAE → any | 0% | No WHT |

## For US Citizens Specifically

US DTAs don't help you avoid US tax (you owe US tax on worldwide income regardless). But they help with:
- **Foreign Tax Credit (FTC)**: Credit foreign taxes paid against US liability
- **Avoiding double taxation**: DTA ensures you don't pay full tax in both countries
- **Treaty tie-breaker**: If two countries both claim you as tax resident, DTA determines which one wins

## When DTAs DON'T Help

1. **Pass-through entities**: US LLC income isn't subject to DTA WHT rates
2. **Tax havens with no DTAs**: Cayman, BVI have few/no DTAs
3. **Same-country transactions**: DTA only applies cross-border
4. **Below treaty thresholds**: Small payments often below minimum for withholding

## Bottom Line for Solo Founders

- **Using US LLC?** → DTAs barely matter (pass-through)
- **Using Singapore/HK?** → 0% WHT on dividends regardless of DTA
- **Using Estonia OÜ?** → EU distributions are DTA-free; non-EU check rates
- **Using Dubai?** → 0% everything, DTAs are bonus insurance
- **Living in high-tax country?** → DTA ensures you get credit for corporate tax paid

## Specific Treaty Examples

### How to Read These Examples

Each example follows the same pattern: who you are, what the situation is, how the treaty tie-breaker resolves, and what the practical tax outcome looks like. These are illustrative — actual outcomes depend on specific facts.

### US–UK Treaty

**Scenario:** US citizen who has moved to the UK. UK residence established: long-term rental flat, UK employment income, spouse and children in the UK, UK bank accounts.

| Tie-Breaker Step | Resolution |
|------------------|-----------|
| Step 1: Permanent home | Permanent home available in UK (rented flat). No US home available (prior US accommodation given up). → **Resolves at Step 1: UK resident for treaty purposes.** |

**Tax outcome:**

| Obligation | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Treaty position | UK has primary taxing rights on employment/business income |
| US obligation | US citizen still files US return on worldwide income — the treaty does not eliminate this |
| Relief mechanism | Foreign Tax Credit (FTC): UK taxes paid credited against US tax liability on the same income |
| FEIE alternative | Available if bona fide foreign residence established; covers up to ~$130K (2025) of earned income |
| Form 8833 | Required if claiming treaty position on US return |

> **Key point:** The treaty resolves the residency conflict cleanly at Step 1, but the US retains taxing rights as a matter of citizenship. FTC eliminates most double taxation in practice, but not all — particularly on income types with different characterization in each country.

### US–Singapore Treaty

**Scenario:** US citizen operating a SaaS business, living in Singapore as a tax resident, earning $200K/year.

**Treaty note:** The US–Singapore DTA is **narrower than most OECD model treaties**. Coverage of self-employment income and business profits is limited. Verify whether your specific income type falls under the treaty's articles before assuming coverage.

**Illustrative tax calculation:**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Total income | $200,000 |
| Singapore tax (~12.5% effective rate on $200K) | ~$25,000 |
| FEIE exclusion (2025) | ~$130,000 |
| Income remaining after FEIE | $70,000 |
| US tax on $70K (marginal ~22–24%) | ~$15,400 |
| FTC available for Singapore tax on $70K portion | ~$8,750 |
| Estimated net US tax after FTC | ~$6,650 |
| **Total combined tax** | **~$31,650 (~16% effective rate)** |

> **Practical reality:** A $200K earner faces meaningful US tax even after FEIE and FTC. Singapore's low rate does not translate to a near-zero combined rate for US citizens. The FEIE covers only the first $130K of earned income; the remainder is subject to US tax with limited FTC offset depending on income sourcing.

### US–Portugal Treaty

**Scenario:** US citizen who moved to Portugal under the NHR (Non-Habitual Resident) regime.

**NHR status note:** Portugal's NHR regime as originally designed is now closed (abolished for new applicants from 2024). This example covers the mechanics for those who qualified under the original NHR rules and remains illustrative for understanding treaty interaction with preferential regimes.

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| Treaty basis | US–Portugal DTA follows the OECD model closely |
| NHR tax on PT-source self-employment income | 20% flat rate (for qualifying professions) |
| NHR treatment of certain foreign-source income | Exempt in Portugal under NHR (certain categories of foreign-source income were excluded entirely) |
| FTC interaction | Where Portugal exempts foreign-source income under NHR rather than taxing it, the US FTC is **not available** — there is no Portuguese tax paid to credit |
| Result | US citizen on NHR with foreign-source SaaS income: Portugal taxes at 0% (exemption), US taxes at full US rates with no FTC offset |

**Key mechanic:** The NHR exemption method (as opposed to the credit method) eliminates the foreign tax that would otherwise generate FTC. This is counterintuitive: a preferential zero-tax treatment in Portugal can *increase* total combined tax for a US citizen compared to Portugal simply taxing the income at a low rate.

### Summary Comparison

| Treaty Pair | Tie-Breaker Outcome | US Filing Required? | Primary Relief Mechanism | Watch Out For |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|--------------------------|---------------|
| US–UK | Resolves at Step 1 (permanent home in UK) | Yes — worldwide income | FTC / FEIE | Different income characterization UK vs US |
| US–Singapore | Often resolves at Step 1 or 2 | Yes — worldwide income | FEIE (first $130K) + limited FTC | Narrow treaty; self-employment coverage limited |
| US–Portugal (NHR) | Resolves under treaty; NHR affects rate | Yes — worldwide income | FTC only where PT actually taxes; FEIE | NHR exemption eliminates FTC on exempt income |
